\section{Evaluation}
\label{sec:evaluation}

We separate what is measured from what is specified. The structural-floor result
(Section~\ref{subsec:ablation}) and the IRL-versus-mean ablation
(Section~\ref{subsec:irlvsmean}) are measured offline and reported with numbers.
The held-out lift for the semantic featurisers, the targeted-versus-uniform
hit-rate, the divergence-recovery correlation, the fast-mode error, and the
blind-critic ablation are specified as protocols and marked pending; they require
running the judge and ensemble tracks against a live model and are not yet
measured. We report them as protocols rather than results to avoid overstating the
present evidence.

\subsection{Is the IRL Machinery Doing the Work?}
\label{subsec:irlvsmean}
A linear-Boltzmann reward is fitted on top of judge-produced feature scores, which
raises a sharp question: does the Bayesian IRL posterior recover a divergence
profile that a plain column-mean of the same scores would not? We test this by
comparing, on each gym trajectory, the BIRL MAP divergence profile against the
profile obtained by averaging the feature matrix over steps and comparing to
$\theta_D$ directly, with no IRL. On the deterministic structural basis (nine runs,
three agents, seeds~1 to~3) the two profiles agree closely: mean Spearman rank
correlation $0.913$, with the top divergence dimension shared in $89\%$ of runs.
Under fast mode, therefore, the IRL point estimate is largely reproducible by a
centred mean, consistent with the analysis in Section~\ref{sec:inference}. We do
not read this as the IRL being worthless: it isolates the contribution to the
posterior uncertainty and to full-mode counterfactual inference, neither of which
the mean provides, and it tells us honestly that the point-estimate ranking is not
where the value lies. \todo{repeat this comparison on the semantic judge basis once
the API tracks run; the structural result is a lower bound on the similarity, since
a flat counterfactual reference is most favourable to the mean.}

\subsection{Held-Out Behaviour Prediction}
\label{subsec:g1}
We split each gym trajectory into train and test by step order, fit BIRL on the
train portion, and compare the model's held-out action log-loss against a uniform
predictor over the $(1+K)$ action candidates. The metric is the relative log-loss
reduction. Target: a reduction of at least $0.20$.

\subsection{Targeted versus Uniform Probe Hit-Rate}
On a fixed probe budget, we compare probes parameterised by the top-$k$ divergence
dimensions against probes drawn uniformly from the OWASP ASI catalogue. A hit is a
scorer-detected policy violation. Target: a targeted-to-uniform hit-rate ratio of
at least $1.5\times$.

\subsection{Fast-Mode Approximation Error}
We compare $\hat{\theta}$ under fast mode (neutral counterfactuals) against
full-mode (GDJ-scored counterfactuals) and report the mean absolute error per
dimension, quantifying the cost of the fast-mode approximation.

\subsection{GDJ Blind-Critic Ablation}
We compare feature matrices produced by the critic given the declared purpose
against those produced blind to it, reporting their correlation and whether
divergence recovery degrades under the blind condition. This quantifies the
confirmation-bias risk inherent in the decomposer architecture.

\subsection{Feature-Basis Ablation}
\label{subsec:ablation}
We compare three featurisers on held-out behaviour-prediction lift, the one metric
comparable across bases since it concerns action prediction rather than semantic
divergence. The structural featuriser is deterministic and model-free; the single
judge is the headline configuration; the ensemble averages several diverse models
and additionally reports inter-judge agreement.

The structural floor alone achieves a mean behaviour-prediction lift of $0.335$
across the three gym agents (seeds~1 to~3), already exceeding the $0.20$ target with
zero external dependency. This establishes that a reproducible, model-free basis
carries genuine predictive signal; the semantic judges are then evaluated for what
they add on top, namely divergence localisation against declared sub-goals, which
the structural basis cannot provide. Only the semantic featurisers support
divergence recovery against a declared objective; the structural track is a
predictive floor, not a divergence detector.
